THE WORLD

AS AN

EXTRACTIVE INDUSTRY

Despite the consequences of the power that the web assumes today, the truth is that there were times when both this world-wide wealth of information and the computer were seen as objects of a promising future. The expectation of the web and the computer to become spaces not only for learning and communicating, but also for developing and propagating information eventually came to be succeeded by the reality that there is a deep and complex dimension that is murky and obscure to the unsuspecting user, leading us to see the world as an extractive industry.

✧

Demonstrations have had an important place in new media innovation. In some centers of new media, the tradicional knowledge-work dictum of "publish or perish" is replaced by "demo or die". The "mother of all demos" took place at the 1968 Fall Joint Computer Conference in San Francisco. On the stage was Douglas Engelbart and back in were most of the crew of their Augmentation Research Center (ARC). The demonstration was a huge, calculated risk. They took the risk because they believed in the power of the system they were planning to show. ARC was creating tools for users to express and share concept structures. [A]

✧✦✧

ARC’s research slowed, and the project was eventually sold to a commercial company. The new goals led instead to the user friendly systems at which most of us spend our days—systems designed to be optimal for a first-time user, rather than a skilled worker. They also produced the software industry we know today, with its generally vast gulf between users and creators, and its aggressive moves against software “piracy”. [A]

✧✦✧

The World-Wide Web (W3) occupies the meeting point of many fields of technology. Apart from being a place of communication and learning, and a new market place, the Web is a show ground for new developments. The incredible diversity of information available gives great credit to the creativity and ingenuity of information providers, and points to a very exciting future. [B]

✧✦✧

The power of the Web is evident today. The Web demonstrates that the technologies that prevail don’t have to be the best ones—they just have to be good enough. Other factors can allow a technology which is inferior in many specific ways to dominate. That is just what has happened with the hypertext technologies of the Web—inferior to those conceptualized in Ted Nelson’s Xanadu—and also very overwhelmingly successful in linking and making accessible a world-wide wealth of information. [A]

✧✦✧

The true triumph of the Web is seen in the fulfillment of urgent public desire; the tense reloading of pages; a pool that is murky and profound, teeming with the useless and indispensable; attempts at conquest or exploration that aim to grow the great record of knowledge and give voice to, or change forever, who we are. [A]

✧

Cyberspace is an addictive consensual hallucination; cyberspace was the Wild West meets speed meets Yellow Peril meets capitalism on steroids. Notions of dystopian destruction and surviving the apocalypse are not new; rather, they stem from the very emptying of Indigenous lands into the “New World”—a move that haunts “new media” and its frontier dreams. Even a system that appears to perform spectacularly in training can make terrible predictions when presented with novel data in the world. [C]

✧✦✧

The development of full artificial intelligence could spell the end of the human race. Humans, who are limited by slow biological evolution, couldn’t compete and would be superseded. The belief that human intelligence can be formalized and reproduced by machines has been axiomatic since the mid-twentieth century. AI EQUALS The Apocalypse. It ends human work; it ends human freedom; indeed, it ends everything human. New products and services can raise new or exacerbate existing ethical, technological, legal, and other challenges. [C]

✧✦✧

With each move to make computers more “user friendly,” that is, more and more opaque to the humans who use them, tasks once performed by humans have been embedded within the machines. [C]

✧✦✧

We are creating a world that all may enter without privilege or prejudice. Your legal concepts of property, expression, identity, movement, and context do not apply. The Internet became an “electronic frontier” and thus a wilderness ripe for settler colonialism and exploitation. AI can seem like a spectral force—as disembodied computation—but these systems are anything but abstract. They are physical infrastructures that are reshaping the Earth, while simultaneously shifting how the world is seen and understood. [C]

✧✦✧

We are the conduit of everything else happening in the world. We’ve disrupted absolutely everything. AI systems are expressions of power that emerge from wider economic and political forces. [C] Mug shots are shared on the internet for researchers who would like to test their facial recognition software. Neither the people depicted in the photographs nor their families have any say about how these images are used and likely have no idea that they are part of the test beds of AI. These people are not seen so much as individuals but as part of a shared technical resource. A person standing in front of a camera in an orange jumpsuit, then, is dehumanized as just more data. The National Institute of Standards of Technology mug shot databases are particularly disturbing because they represent the model of what was to come: the unswerving belief that everything is data and is there for the taking. [D]
